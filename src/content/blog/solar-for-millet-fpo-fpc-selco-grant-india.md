India is the world's largest producer of millets — jowar, bajra, ragi, foxtail millet, little millet, kodo and more. The UN declared 2023 the International Year of Millets. Demand from health-conscious consumers, urban retail, and export markets has never been higher.

Farmer Producer Companies (FPCs) and FPOs across Maharashtra, Karnataka, Telangana, Rajasthan and Madhya Pradesh are scaling up — sourcing millets from smallholder farmers, cleaning, grading, dehulling, milling and packaging for retail and institutional buyers.

And then the electricity bill arrives.

A mid-sized millet processing unit on an industrial tariff pays **₹50,000 to ₹1.2 lakh per month** on electricity alone. That single line item can make the difference between a profitable FPO and one that cannot sustain itself — or pass fair prices back to the farming members it exists to serve.

Solar is the most direct fix. And the economics are unusually strong.

## Why Millets and Solar Are a Natural Fit

Not all agri-processing loads suit solar equally well. Millet processing is one of the best matches — for three structural reasons.

### 1. Daytime Operations Align with Solar Generation

Millet grading, de-stoning, pulverising, dehulling and packaging runs during daylight hours. This is when solar generates at full output. Unlike a cold storage that needs power at night, a millet processing unit consumes most of its electricity exactly when solar is producing it.

The result: you use solar power directly, in real time, at full value — without needing battery storage.

### 2. The Load Profile Is Predictable

Millet processing equipment — graders, conveyors, blowers, pulverisers, packaging machines — draws steady, predictable motor loads. This makes system sizing accurate. You get a precise generation-to-consumption match, not a rough estimate.

### 3. Industrial Tariffs Make Every Unit Saved Highly Valuable

Most millet processing FPOs fall under MSEDCL LT-V, BESCOM LT-5, or equivalent state industrial tariff categories — with effective rates of ₹14–20/unit. That means every unit generated by solar is worth ₹14–20 in avoided cost. At those rates, even a modestly sized system produces substantial monthly savings.

This pattern is typical for millet FPOs: a low off-season (Feb–May when millet stocks are thin) and a heavy peak season (Jun–Nov after kharif harvest). The variability makes electricity budgeting difficult and procurement pricing unstable.

Solar eliminates the energy charge component — which is the largest variable portion of the bill.

## What a Solar System Does to These Numbers

A **45 kW on-grid solar system** generates approximately 7,400 kWh/month under Satara's 5.5 peak sun hours — enough to offset 100% of the baseline monthly consumption (4,012 kWh). Surplus generation in low-demand months is exported to the grid via net metering and credited against future bills.

| Parameter | Before Solar | After Solar |
|---|---|---|
| Monthly energy bill | ₹74,630 | ~₹13,600 (fixed demand only) |
| Energy charges | ₹47,856/month | ₹0 |
| Net monthly saving | — | **~₹61,000** |
| Annual saving | — | **~₹7,32,000** |
| CO₂ avoided | — | ~61 tonnes/year |

The fixed demand charge (₹13,600/month in this case) cannot be eliminated by solar — it is linked to contracted demand, not units consumed. But the energy charge, which is the largest and most volatile component, is entirely offset.

## How Millet FPOs Fund the Solar System

A ₹17–18 lakh solar system is not a trivial investment for a rural FPC. Several funding routes exist:

### Government Capital Subsidy
The PM Surya Ghar scheme and state-level DISCOM programmes offer capital subsidies primarily for residential solar, but agri-processing units may qualify under certain state schemes. Check with your state DISCOM or state renewable energy agency (MEDA in Maharashtra, KREDL in Karnataka, etc.).

### NABARD Loans and RIDF
NABARD's Rural Infrastructure Development Fund and agricultural lending windows offer subsidised financing for agri-processing infrastructure, including solar. Interest rates are typically 2–4% below market. Many FPCs are already NABARD-registered — making this a natural starting point.

### Clean Energy Grants for FPOs
Several grant-making bodies fund solar for rural enterprises and FPOs:

- **SELCO Foundation** — funds solar for underserved rural entrepreneurs and women-led enterprises. Grants of 40–50% of project cost for qualifying FPOs with strong social impact profiles
- **GIZ (Indo-German development cooperation)** — cluster-level rural energy interventions, often in partnership with state agriculture departments
- **Gram Vikas** — smaller grants for tribal and marginalised community enterprises
- **CSR Funding** — large agri-input and FMCG companies increasingly fund rural supply chain solar as part of CSR and ESG mandates

For Mandprabha FPC, a SELCO Foundation grant of **₹7,64,180** (43% of cost) was secured — cutting payback from ~2.5 years to **16 months**.

### Own Funds
For FPOs with accumulated reserves, self-financing is the simplest route — no documentation, no waiting, and the full benefit from day one.

## What to Look for in a Solar Partner for Your Millet FPC

Solar for agri-processing is different from rooftop solar for homes. A millet FPC needs a partner who understands:

**Load profile analysis** — your consumption swings from 450 kWh in February to 9,000+ kWh in August. The system must be sized for the right baseline, not the peak, with net metering handling the surplus.

**Industrial tariff structure** — ToD (Time of Day) tariffs in Maharashtra and other states have different peak and off-peak rates. System sizing needs to optimise against your specific tariff slab.

**DISCOM compliance** — MSEDCL, BESCOM, TSSPDCL and other DISCOMs have specific technical standards for LT industrial solar connections. Your installer must know the interconnection requirements.

**Grant documentation** — if you are pursuing a grant, the financial model, impact report and application must be prepared in the format the grant body requires. A generic solar quote does not qualify.

**O&M continuity** — a millet processing unit operates year-round. Your solar system needs annual maintenance, inverter health monitoring, and generation reporting — not just installation and departure.
